Top 3 Reasons to Vote No

The county's own record tells a damning story. These are the facts voters need most before June.

1

The Board corrected its own $300M figure — and voted anyway

County staff originally posted that Contra Costa faced $307 million in ongoing annual losses. Supervisor Andersen, the lone no vote, flagged the error before the vote: county staff confirmed the figure is a cumulative 4-year total, later revised down to $229 million. Framing it as annual makes the funding gap appear 7–8× larger than it is in the near term. The board voted to place Measure B on the ballot the same day, without correcting the record.

2

Most HR1 provisions don't kick in until 2027 or 2028

Work requirements begin in January 2027. Six-month eligibility redeterminations begin in 2027. DSH hospital funding cuts were extended by Congress through September 2028. Measure B would start collecting taxes in October 2026 — locking in five years of higher taxes before the full picture is even known.

3

DSH hospital cuts predate HR1 by 15 years and have been delayed 12+ times

The Disproportionate Share Hospital cuts the county cites were mandated by the Affordable Care Act in 2010 — not HR1. Congress has delayed them on a bipartisan basis more than a dozen times across four presidential administrations. The latest delay, signed February 2026, extends them to September 2028.

Things you should know about Measure B

These points are based on documents and published reporting. They focus on tax rates, timing, and the county's own rollout of Measure B.

1

Measure B adds five-eighths of a cent countywide

Measure B would increase the county sales tax by 0.625%. Based on current California Department of Tax and Fee Administration rates, that would push the total sales tax to 10.875% in the county's highest-tax cities.

2

Another countywide sales tax may be coming in November

State law already authorizes a regional transit tax measure for the November 2026 ballot, and planning documents describe a 0.5% increase for Contra Costa County if that measure goes forward.

3

Supervisors proceeded after the projected loss number was revised down

On March 3, published coverage reported that supervisors were told the projected federal-and-state funding loss estimate should be revised downward, yet the board still voted to place Measure B on the ballot that day.

A legal challenge is already filed

Voters deserve to know that Measure B faces serious legal questions — not just political opposition.

Active Litigation

The Lawsuit: Case No. N26-0456

Case No. N26-0456

A legal challenge to Measure B has been filed in Contra Costa County Superior Court. The complaint argues that the county failed to follow proper procedures in placing the measure on the ballot, and that voters were presented with a misleading funding picture.

Legal challenges to ballot measures are relatively rare — typically brought only when attorneys believe there is a substantial legal defect. If the court finds for the plaintiffs, the tax could be invalidated even if voters approve it. That would mean residents paid higher taxes for a period only to have the revenue returned or the program unwound.

We will post a link to the filed complaint here when available. Sign up below to stay informed as the case develops.

Plain English

The Sales Tax Cap Issue

California law imposes a combined local sales tax cap of 2% above the state base rate. Several Contra Costa cities are already at or near that limit. Adding Measure B's 0.625% could push them over the cap.

Under current California law, a city at the cap cannot legally collect additional taxes beyond it without state legislation authorizing an exception — legislation that has not passed and is not guaranteed to pass before the June election.

In plain terms: voters could approve Measure B, but the tax might be legally unenforceable in some cities until Sacramento acts. The county has not clearly disclosed this risk to voters in its ballot materials.

Why it matters

Even before any fall measures, Measure B would make everyday purchases more expensive across Contra Costa County.

What is on the table

  • Measure B is a general sales tax — revenue goes to the county general fund with no dedicated purpose.
  • The county's own ballot language says the tax would run for five years.
  • The county estimated the measure would raise about $150 million annually.
  • The earliest collection date in county materials is October 1, 2026 — before most HR1 impacts are felt.
  • A legal challenge (Case No. N26-0456) is pending in Contra Costa County Superior Court.

Key questions voters should ask

Before voting yes on any tax measure, it is reasonable to ask:

  • Why did the county present a $307M annual figure that turned out to be a 4-year cumulative total?
  • Why lock in a 5-year tax before federal and state rules are even finalized?
  • Is the sales tax cap issue resolved for all cities in the county?
  • What happens to tax revenue if the legal challenge (N26-0456) succeeds?
  • Have alternatives — including the November state ballot — been fully assessed?
